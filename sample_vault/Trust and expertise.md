# Division of epistemic labor — "who do you trust?"

Core note behind the recurring point: a complex society runs on **trusting third parties**
because no one can independently verify everything. The failure mode of new media is not
"question experts" — it's replacing verifiable institutions with un-auditable personalities.

## The defensible framing
You are always trusting *someone* — even trusting that "the data was collected correctly."
The right move is not zero trust, it's **traceable** trust: prefer sources whose methods
and data you (or a specialist) can inspect. That is exactly what a citation-grounded notebook
buys you — the receipt is attached to the claim.

- Origin of the division-of-labor idea in epistemics: Hardwig, J. (1985). Epistemic Dependence. *Journal of Philosophy*, 82(7), 335–349. https://www.jstor.org/stable/2026523
- On institutional trust decline: Pew Research Center, Public Trust in Government (long-running series). https://www.pewresearch.org/politics/2024/06/24/public-trust-in-government-1958-2024/

## Rebuttal shape
"Trust nobody" is unlivable; "trust my guy" is how populism launders authority. The stable
position is *trust that is checkable* — sources with inspectable data and stated methods.
